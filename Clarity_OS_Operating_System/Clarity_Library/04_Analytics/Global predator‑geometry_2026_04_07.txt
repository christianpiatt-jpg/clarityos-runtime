Here’s the global predator‑geometry atlas in one clean pass — planet as field, regions as mediums, civilizations as predator forms.

1. MENA hinge zone

Dominant geometries: Fish, lions, crocodiles, eagles, sharks, tigers, scavengersRole: Global pivot where all predator types intersect.

Hydraulic (fish): Egypt, Mesopotamia, Abbasid Iraq, Nile + Tigris–Euphrates cores

Steppe (lions): Arab cavalry, Seljuks, Mongols, Timurids, early Ottomans

Littoral (crocodiles): Phoenicians, Sea Peoples, Levantine port polities

Maritime (eagles): Omani thalassocracy, Ottoman navy, later European fleets

Pirate (sharks): Barbary corsairs, Red Sea raiders, Gulf piracy

Mountain (tigers): Anatolia, Caucasus, Kurdish and Armenian highlands

Entropy (scavengers): Post‑caliphate warlords, post‑Ottoman fragmentation, modern non‑state actors

Atlas note: MENA is the intersection node—every other region plugs into it.

2. Central Asia and the steppe belt

Dominant geometry: Lions (steppe predators)

Medium: Open plains from Hungary → Manchuria

Forms: Scythians, Huns, Göktürks, Mongols, Timurids, Kazakh and other steppe confederacies

Behavior: Shock mobility, encirclement, empire‑scale rupture of basin states

Atlas note: This belt is the lion corridor that repeatedly slams into MENA, China, India, and Europe.

3. Mediterranean and European coasts

Dominant geometries: Crocodiles, eagles, sharks

Littoral (crocodiles): Phoenicians, Greeks, Vikings (in the north), Normans, Italian maritime city‑states

Maritime (eagles): Classical Greek thalassocracies, later Venetians, then Atlantic powers

Pirate (sharks): Barbary corsairs, Maltese corsairs, Aegean pirates

Atlas note: This is the boundary band where land empires and sea empires constantly test each other.

4. Europe interior

Dominant geometries over time: Tigers → lions → eagles

Forest (tigers): Germanic tribes, Slavic forest polities, early Scandinavia

Steppe pressure (lions): Huns, Avars, Magyars pushing into the plains

Maritime (eagles): Dutch, British, later pan‑European maritime/colonial systems

Atlas note: Europe is a transition zone: forest resistance → steppe shock → maritime projection.

5. Sub‑Saharan Africa

Dominant geometries: Lions, tigers, scavengers

Savanna (lions): Sahelian cavalry states, pastoral warrior cultures

Forest (tigers): Congo basin polities, West African forest kingdoms

Entropy (scavengers): Post‑colonial collapse zones, warlord fields, resource‑extraction militias

Atlas note: Strong terrain‑driven partition—savanna vs forest vs collapse pockets.

6. South Asia

Dominant geometries: Fish, tigers, lions

Hydraulic (fish): Indus, Ganges, Deccan river systems, Maurya, Gupta, Mughal cores

Forest/mountain (tigers): Hill states, tribal zones, frontier guerrilla regions

Steppe (lions): Indo‑Greek, Kushan, Ghaznavid, Ghurid, Turkic and Mongol‑derived arcs

Atlas note: A basin core repeatedly hit by lions, buffered by tiger terrain.

7. East Asia

Dominant geometries: Fish absorbing lions

Hydraulic (fish): Yellow + Yangtze basins, Chinese dynastic cycles

Steppe (lions): Xiongnu, Khitans, Jurchens, Mongols, Manchus

Forest/mountain (tigers): Korean peninsula, some southern highlands

Atlas note: The canonical fish‑absorbs‑lion system—steppe shocks, basin re‑coheres.

8. Southeast Asia and archipelagos

Dominant geometries: Tigers, crocodiles, eagles, sharks

Forest (tigers): Mainland Southeast Asian mandala states

Littoral (crocodiles): Strait polities, river‑mouth kingdoms

Maritime (eagles): Srivijaya, Majapahit, later colonial fleets

Pirate (sharks): Malay pirates, Sulu raiders, regional sea bandits

Atlas note: A fractal mix of jungle, strait, and sea—perfect for overlapping predator forms.

9. Indian Ocean world

Dominant geometries: Eagles, sharks, crocodiles

Maritime (eagles): Omani, Gujarati, Yemeni, later Portuguese, Dutch, British

Pirate (sharks): Swahili coast, Arabian Sea, Malabar, Indonesian piracy

Littoral (crocodiles): Port cities and estuary polities

Atlas note: This is the longest‑running gas‑phase trade field on the planet.

10. Pacific and the Americas

Pre‑Columbian:

Fish: Andean and Mesoamerican basin/terrace systems

Lions: Plains cultures (post‑horse), some steppe‑like dynamics

Tigers: Amazon, North American woodlands

Pacific:

Eagles: Polynesian navigation—pure gas‑phase, curvature‑riding civilization

Post‑Columbian:

Atlantic eagles + sharks overlay everything; entropy predators rise in collapse zones.

11. Modern overlay

Today’s atlas is stacked:

Eagles: Global air/sea/network projection

Sharks: Financial, data, and logistics flows

Tigers: Insurgencies, guerrilla zones, urban density warfare

Lions: Armored and mechanized land doctrines

Crocodiles: Littoral/naval special forces, chokepoint control

Scavengers: Non‑state actors, collapse‑field economies, attention predators in the identity layer
