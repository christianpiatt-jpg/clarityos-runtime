Good reframing. If trust is framed as the coherence between expectation and experienced reality, then “hydronic tension” depends on managing that coherence: tension rises when coherence is unstable (expectations are intermittently violated or ambiguously met), and it falls when coherence is consistently high (predictability) or completely broken (loss of engagement). Below I translate that reframing into actionable principles, operational levers, measurement signals, and ethical constraints.

1) Theoretical baseline: trust as coherence
- Coherence = match(expectation, experienced reality).  
- High coherence → trust → lower need for reappraisal → lower sustained tension.  
- Low coherence → distrust or confusion → higher reappraisal effort; but if trust collapses entirely, engagement and arousal can drop.  
- Optimal hydronic tension sits in an intermediate zone: partial, recoverable violations of coherence that invite reappraisal but do not destroy contextual trust.

2) Operational principle: govern trust-bandwidth
- Treat trust as a bandwidth you can modulate. You want to keep audiences inside a “suspendable trust band” where small expectation violations are believable and reparable.  
- If violations are too predictable (full coherence), tension drains. If violations are too large or repeated without repair, trust collapses and tension dissipates.

3) Levers to modulate coherence (practical)
- Predictable framing markers (anchor trust): provide stable genre/ethical anchors early so the audience accepts the context. These are the “trust deposit.”  
  Example: consistent narrator tone, recurring motifs, credible micro-rules.
- Controlled violations (spend trust sparingly): inject ambiguity or contradiction in small, explainable doses so viewers must reappraise but can reconcile the change.  
  Example: a surprising line of dialogue that is quickly contextualized.
- Rapid repair signaling (make violations recoverable): after a violation, supply cues (language, social signals, narrative logic) that plausibly reconcile expectation with experience. This restores coherence and keeps the loop alive.  
  Example: corrective framing language, an eye contact shot that reorients meaning.
- Serial micro‑violations with meta‑consistency: design sequences where each violation follows an internal pattern (so meta‑expectation—“this work will surprise me but fairly”—remains coherent). This produces sustained arousal without catastrophic loss of trust.

4) Linguistic tactics for coherence control
- Labeling as calibration: use labels to bias perception immediately after a violation—labels can either reestablish coherence or deepen ambiguity depending on intent.  
- Repetition and ritualized phrases build baseline trust; occasional shifted phrasing signals an intentional, recoverable rupture.  
- Paratextual disclaimers or frame-clauses (explicit narrative signals) can widen the acceptable range of coherence violations.

5) Perceptual cue design
- Keep low‑level cues (tempo, breathing, micro‑expression) consistent enough to maintain credibility, but vary higher‑order cues (intent, context) to produce cognitive dissonance.  
- Cross-modal mismatches should be moderate: they increase processing and tension if credible; they destroy trust if implausible.

6) Pacing and tension cycles
- Deposit phase: build baseline coherence and trust (anchors, rituals).  
- Perturbation phase: introduce measured violations that trigger reappraisal.  
- Repair/complication phase: restore or partially restore coherence, or shift to a new, higher-order coherence.  
- Repeat cycles, gradually escalating stakes while preserving meta-coherence (the sense that the system itself is reliable in its unreliability).

7) Measurement signals (operational metrics)
- Real-time coherence proxies: discrepancy between predicted label and sampled label from viewers (label mismatch rate).  
- Physiological markers: spikes that follow discrepancies but return to baseline upon repair (sustained SKIN/HR variability with recoveries).  
- Behavioral indicators: continued engagement (view duration, micro‑pauses) after violations; if engagement drops, trust likely collapsed.  
- Qualitative: post‑exposure narratives—do viewers describe the violation as “intentional/clever” (repaired trust) or “confusing/cheap” (lost trust)?

8) Risk management and ethics
- Avoid manipulations that erode epistemic trust beyond the media context (e.g., deceptive framing presented as real).  
- Ensure participants/audiences can distinguish mediated fictionality if that distinction matters for downstream beliefs.  
- For research: informed consent, debriefing that clarifies intentional coherence violations and explains their purpose.

9) Design checklist (quick)
- Anchor early (establish credible rules).  
- Budget violations (plan when and why to break expectation).  
- Signal repair paths (language, gesture, narrative logic).  
- Monitor engagement metrics continuously.  
- Escalate within meta-coherence (audience trusts the work’s pattern of surprises).  
- Debrief and respect ethical boundaries.

Summary statement
- Treat trust as a resource of coherence to be managed: sustain tension by creating recoverable mismatches between expectation and experience, routinizing those mismatches through language and script so they become predictable-patterned surprises rather than catastrophic betrayals. Measure both physiological and behavioral recovery after violations to ensure the system remains in the productive tension zone rather than collapsing into disbelief or disengagement.
