Q: Some heirs are saying thanks but no thanks to inheritances. Why?




Reporter Laura Saunders wrote about a useful tax-planning tool that some older savers may want to consider. 





A: It’s called a disclaimer. In essence, it is a strategic rejection of an inherited asset by an heir. The result is that part or all of the inheritance passes to someone else, often a child or grandchild. The move can save taxes and accomplish other goals for families. It can be used with IRAs and also other inherited assets.

I’m betting many older savers are overlooking disclaimers as an IRA tax-planning tool. I was one of them. However, I recently disclaimed part of a traditional IRA I inherited, and it worked out well. My adult children received a legacy from a relative they loved and the overall taxes will be lower.

As a result, I’m struck by how useful disclaimers can be for retirees and pre-retirees who have traditional IRAs far larger than they expected. Thanks to diligent saving, 401(k) rollovers, and market growth, these accounts are often a retiree’s largest asset. While this is a great problem to have, it poses dilemmas for savers trying to minimize income taxes.

Q: Is moving abroad the way to find your dream job?







People frustrated by political and job-market disruptions think the grass might be greener elsewhere. Columnist Callum Borchers explores the benefits and drawbacks of an international career move:







A: The short answer is probably not. Moving abroad is one of those things people like to dream and talk about. Following through is another story.

Still, it’s striking that so many Americans are even thinking about this. STEM professionals, in particular, say jobs in government, academia and even the private sector feel less secure because of federal shutdowns and funding cuts. Foreign countries are actively wooing these workers.

I recently attended an event where a contingent from Finland pitched a Nordic escape to science and tech types. Most people I met weren’t ready to take the (very cold) plunge. Common hangups include settling for a lower salary overseas, learning a new language and losing your professional network.

Some told me they will get over those barriers if their professional prospects worsen at home. And one man who works in sales said he will relocate to Finland in May. He’ll be part of a net out-migration from the U.S. this year, according to a projection by the Brookings Institution.

Q: A simple test can assess your risk of heart disease. Should you get one?




Measuring cholesterol levels has long been the main way doctors assess the risk. Increasingly, people are also opting for a simple, relatively affordable test: a coronary artery calcium scan. Health reporter Sumathi Reddy lays out what you need to know.





A: These scans have been around for decades, but they got a boost from influential clinical guidelines issued earlier this month by leading cardiology groups.

The tests, which take less than 10 minutes, show if there are calcium deposits in the heart, which is a proxy for plaque. If paying out-of-pocket, the scans typically cost about $75 to $200, says Dr. Michael Blaha, a professor of cardiology at the Ciccarone Center for the Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease at Johns Hopkins Medicine, although they are increasingly covered by insurance.

Not everyone needs a calcium scan. If you’re middle-aged and not already on a cholesterol-lowering medication but have risk factors for heart disease, you fall into the bucket of people who should get one.

Is there a downside to getting a scan? One legitimate concern among cardiologists, says Blaha, is doctors who may inappropriately use a calcium score to justify doing a procedure, such as putting a stent in.
