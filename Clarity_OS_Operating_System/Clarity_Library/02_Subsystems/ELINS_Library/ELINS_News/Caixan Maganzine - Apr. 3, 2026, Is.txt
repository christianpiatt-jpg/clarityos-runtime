China, Pakistan Propose Five-Point Peace Plan to End Gulf Conflict
Apr 02, 2026 02:57 AM
00:00/02:21
Listen to this article
Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and Pakistani Deputy Prime Minister and Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar met in Beijing on March 31. Photo: Chinese Foreign Ministry
Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and Pakistani Deputy Prime Minister and Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar met in Beijing on March 31. Photo: Chinese Foreign Ministry
China and Pakistan have laid out a five-point peace initiative aimed at halting the escalating conflict in the Middle East and the Gulf region, demanding an immediate ceasefire and the rapid restoration of commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. 

Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and Pakistani Deputy Prime Minister and Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar presented the joint proposal during a meeting in Beijing on March 31, according to a statement released by the Chinese Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Wang and Dar exchanged views on the regional crisis before outlining the following framework.

China and Pakistan called for an immediate ceasefire to stop the conflict from spreading. The two nations urged all factions to allow humanitarian relief supplies unrestricted access to all areas impacted by the war.

The initiative stated that the sovereignty, territorial integrity, national independence, and security of Iran and the Gulf states must be preserved. Emphasizing that dialogue and diplomacy are “the only viable paths to resolving the conflict,” Beijing and Islamabad voiced support for the involved parties to begin negotiations and commit to a peaceful resolution. Wang and Dar stressed that no party should use — or threaten to use — military force during the peace talks.

Declaring the protection of civilians during military conflicts a principle that cannot be breached, the two countries urged all warring parties to immediately halt attacks on civilians and non-military targets. They called for comprehensive compliance with international humanitarian law and an end to strikes on vital infrastructure — including energy, water desalination, and power facilities — as well as peaceful nuclear plants.

Describing the Strait of Hormuz and its adjacent waters as a crucial international artery for goods and energy, China and Pakistan called on all sides to protect the safety of ships and crews currently stranded in the waterway. The proposal urges authorities to arrange safe transit for civilian and commercial vessels and restore normal navigation in the strait as quickly as possible.

The joint statement advocated for the practice of genuine multilateralism and a strengthened role for the United Nations. Both countries expressed support for reaching an agreement on a comprehensive peace framework and lasting stability based on the purposes and principles of the U.N. Charter and international law.

China and Bahrain Align on U.N. Strategy to Secure Strait of Hormuz
Published: Apr. 2, 2026  3:33 p.m.  GMT+8
00:00/02:35
Listen to this article
Wang Yi and Abdullatif Al Zayani (right). Photo: VCG
Wang Yi and Abdullatif Al Zayani (right). Photo: VCG
Wang Yi, China’s foreign minister and a member of the Communist Party’s Politburo, on Thursday urged international intervention to stabilize the Middle East and safeguard the Strait of Hormuz during an April 2 call with Bahrain Foreign Minister Abdullatif Al Zayani.

Al Zayani, who currently serves as the rotating chairman of the Gulf Cooperation Council, initiated the phone call to brief Wang on the region’s volatile security environment and Bahrain’s strategic posture.

Presenting a grim assessment, Al Zayani warned that Gulf nations face severe security challenges, most notably the blocking of vital shipping lanes in the Strait of Hormuz. He emphasized Bahrain’s intention to resolve the navigation crisis through the United Nations Security Council and requested closer coordination with Beijing.

In response, Wang reiterated China’s principled stance against aggression and its steadfast advocacy for peace. Wang highlighted a recent five-point initiative proposed jointly by China and Pakistan aimed at restoring peace and stability to the Gulf and the broader Middle East. The proposal demands a halt to attacks on civilians and non-military targets, alongside guarantees for the security and resumption of normal traffic through the Strait of Hormuz.

“An immediate cease-fire is the unified demand of the international community,” Wang said. 

He asserted that actions taken by the U.N. Security Council must work toward easing tensions and reviving peace talks. The council must not endorse illegal acts of war, Wang warned, “let alone add fuel to the fire.”

Bahrain initially circulated a U.N. Security Council draft resolution on protecting commercial shipping in and around the Strait of Hormuz. Backed by other Gulf Arab states and the U.S., the draft explicitly invoked Chapter VII of the U.N. Charter, allowing the Security Council to authorize measures ranging from sanctions to military force, according to Reuters.

With Russia and China expected to veto, Bahrain has revised the draft — dropping an explicit reference to binding enforcement while retaining language authorizing “all necessary means,” Reuters reported Wednesday. Diplomats said there is a tentative aim to put the text to a vote on Thursday, according to the report.

As a permanent member of the Security Council and a responsible major power, China is prepared to collaborate with Bahrain to quell the conflict, Wang said. Beijing aims to achieve lasting regional stability and defend the legitimate rights of the Global South — particularly those of small and medium-sized nations.

Chinese anti-graft authorities have placed the head of Zhuhai’s state-owned assets regulator under investigation for severe disciplinary and legal violations, making him the third consecutive chief of the agency to be swept up in a widening corruption probe in the economic powerhouse of Guangdong province. 

The April 2 announcement regarding Li Wenji, who also served as deputy secretary-general of the Zhuhai Municipal Party Committee, comes as investigators unravel a trail of massive public losses tied to local state bailouts of struggling private companies — including a disastrous investment in a supplier to crippled developer China Evergrande.

Multiple informed sources revealed that Li fell almost simultaneously with Guo Yonghang, a member of the party leadership group and vice chairman of the Guangdong provincial political consultative conference. Guo has been placed under investigation for corruption since late March.

On March 8, Li attended a bank-enterprise exchange event. In his remarks, he expressed the official stance that Zhuhai actively supports the bailout of Huafa Group. As the flagship of Zhuhai’s state-owned assets, Huafa is currently mired in a liquidity crisis triggered by the profound downturn in the real estate sector. This was likely the last public appearance Li made before his downfall. 

The 52-year-old previously held executive roles across various companies in Shenzhen, including petroleum trading, commercial storage, environmental engineering, and asset management firms. Most of these were small to micro-sized state-owned enterprises, while a few were purely private companies. Many of these entities have since been deregistered. According to an insider familiar with Shenzhen’s state-owned assets, Li was never a prominent figure within the city’s system. 

In August 2019, Li parachuted into Zhuhai Aviation City Group as its chairman. Formed in May 2009, the group merged with Zhuhai Airport Group three years later — essentially operating as two distinct brands under one management team. While its core businesses include civil aviation transport, general aviation operations, and aviation industrial park development, it also expanded into real estate and municipal engineering. 

When Li took office, Guo was serving as the party secretary of Zhuhai. “Li came highly recommended by Guo,” a source within Zhuhai’s state-owned asset system said. Guo had previously worked in Shenzhen, joining the city’s municipal party standing committee in March 2015 and serving as secretary-general. He was transferred to Zhuhai in February 2018. 

Guo single-handedly pushed forward the reform of Zhuhai’s state-owned assets, successfully completing the restructuring of Gree Electric during his tenure. 

As Zhuhai’s state asset system entered the post-Gree era, the challenge became how to scale up remaining state-owned enterprises to fill the massive void left by Gree’s removal from the balance sheet.

Chinese Drugmakers Shrug Off Broad U.S. Pharmaceutical Tariffs
By Cui XiaotianApr 03, 2026 03:03 AM
00:00/04:53
Listen to this article
A pharmacist takes medication from a shelf at a pharmacy in Provo, Utah, U.S. Photo: VCG
A pharmacist takes medication from a shelf at a pharmacy in Provo, Utah, U.S. Photo: VCG
Chinese pharmaceutical makers are positioned to comfortably evade a sweeping new 100% U.S. tariff on patented drugs, insulated by their reliance on exporting raw materials and a booming strategy of licensing domestic drug rights to overseas partners.

The long-brewing U.S. barrier on pharmaceutical imports has officially landed. In a fact sheet released April 2, the White House announced that Donald Trump is authorizing a 100% tariff on patented drugs and their underlying components. The aggressive levies will go into effect in 120 days for large enterprises, while small and medium-sized businesses have a 180-day grace period. Patented drugs typically encompass original brand-name therapeutics and their modified iterations.

This regulatory net reveals that the immediate impact on Chinese corporations is minimal. China’s pharmaceutical exports to the U.S. are heavily dominated by active pharmaceutical ingredients, which largely skirt the primary tariff barrier. Meanwhile, Chinese makers of innovative therapeutics rely heavily on out-licensing — effectively selling the research, manufacturing, and commercialization rights of their experimental drugs to foreign companies — a strategy that does not trigger the new import taxes.

According to data from the National Medical Products Administration, the total value of out-licensing pharmaceutical deals minted by Chinese firms reached $135.65 billion in 2025. Only a fraction of Chinese corporations directly export patented finished drugs to the U.S. market. BeOne Medicines, one of the few exceptions, officially shifted its corporate registry from the Cayman Islands to Switzerland in 2025, enabling the company to qualify for a much gentler 15% tariff.

Dodging the tariff barrier

The 100% penalty will not apply across the board, as structural exemptions leave numerous foreign drug makers unscathed. First, drugs imported from nations boasting trade agreements with the U.S. — specifically the European Union, Japan, South Korea, Switzerland, and Liechtenstein — will be taxed at 15%. Pharmaceuticals sourced from the United Kingdom will be subject to an even lower rate, hinging on a newly finalized U.S.-U.K. pharmaceutical treaty. The EU, Switzerland, and the U.K. are critical suppliers of patented drugs to the American market.

Second, pharmaceutical operators that pledge to localize manufacturing and execute a “Most-Favored-Nation Price” pact will enjoy zero tariffs until Jan. 20, 2029. Companies that commit only to domestic production, without the pricing agreement, will face a 20% tariff. Manufacturers can strike these deals in parallel with the Department of Health and Human Services and the Department of Commerce.

Third, generic drugs, biosimilars, and their components will not be initially taxed, with the administration planning to re-evaluate their status in a year.

Large multinational pharmaceutical conglomerates have already moved aggressively to digest and mitigate the tariff’s uncertainty. In a bid to secure a three-year window of zero tariffs, 16 out of 17 pharmaceutical giants targeted by Trump in July 2025 have signed Most-Favored-Nation Price agreements. 

Simultaneously, these multinational players have uniformly promised to invest heavily in U.S. domestic manufacturing during the second term of the Trump administration, committing a combined total of more than $400 billion. 

Defending domestic supply chains

The legal backbone for the tariffs stems from the findings of a Section 232 inquiry. In April 2025, under Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962, the Department of Commerce launched an investigation spanning finished drugs, active pharmaceutical ingredients, and broader chemical raw materials. 

The White House fact sheet cited that patented medicines are being imported into the United States in such quantities or under such circumstances as to threaten to impair the national security.

The resulting investigation assessed that, despite the U.S. leading the world in the research and development of most innovative treatments, the American health care system suffers from deep import dependencies. A fracturing of the global supply network — whether sparked by geopolitical tensions or acute economic shocks — could throttle U.S. access to life-saving medicines.

According to the Food and Drug Administration, approximately 53% of all patented drugs sold domestically in 2025 were manufactured abroad. At the raw material level, import reliance is dramatically sharper — domestically produced active pharmaceutical ingredients satisfy only 15% of the U.S. market’s demand for patented medicines.

China Sweeps Cultural Heritage Sector in Widening Anti-Graft Crackdown
By Sun LiangziPublished: Apr. 1, 2026  8:55 p.m.  GMT+8
00:00/04:06
Listen to this article

A high-profile scandal involving the illicit sale of a masterpiece scroll by insiders at one of China’s top museums has prompted Beijing to launch a sweeping nationwide audit of state-owned antiquities, as a widening anti-graft dragnet sweeps through the country's cultural heritage sector.

On April 1, the National Cultural Heritage Administration (NCHA) announced a yearlong, centralized campaign to meticulously inventory state-owned museum holdings and eradicate security vulnerabilities. Following a national teleconference, the agency directed museums to piece-by-piece verify their physical assets against official ledger records, aiming to establish a standardized, institutionalized system for collection management. The initiative will simultaneously act as a pilot for China’s second national census of movable cultural relics.

In its directive, the NCHA urged local regulators and museum administrators to adopt a “correct view on political performance,” using the targeted clampdown to fortify structural safeguards and elevate overall security standards for national treasures.

The regulatory overhaul follows an unprecedented crisis of faith at the Nanjing Museum. In December 2025, massive domestic and international public outcry erupted over the illicit loss of the “Jiangnan Chun” scroll — a priceless 16th-century masterpiece from the prestigious Xuzhai collection that was donated to the state by Pang Zenghe in 1959.

On Feb. 9, a provincial task force supervised by the NCHA revealed that 29 individuals were implicated in the illicit disposal of five donated paintings from the Pang family, including Jiangnan Chun. Aside from five individuals who have since died, authorities placed 24 people under investigation, notably former Nanjing Museum Director Xu Huping.

The fallout has driven an urgent policy pivot in Beijing. At a national museum work conference in December 2025, collection management was elevated to the paramount priority for the sector's upcoming 15th Five-Year Plan. Intervening to address the governance loopholes, the NCHA issued new directives enforcing strict operational protocols for classifying, registering, archiving, and deaccessioning donated artifacts.

The push for tighter compliance is backed by Beijing’s top anti-corruption watchdog. On March 31, the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection (CCDI) published a progress report detailing the NCHA’s rectification efforts following a regular round of central inspections conducted between Nov. 2, 2024, and Jan. 5, 2025. Responding to the structural flaws highlighted in the inspection's April 2025 feedback, the NCHA party leadership pledged to rigorously oversee museum donations and revise the Measures for Museum Collection Management, specifically mandating stringent administrative hurdles for removing any artifact from state collections.

The “Jiangnan Chun” saga unfolds against the backdrop of an aggressive anti-graft cleanup within China’s cultural and museum ecosystem. Over the past two years, an unprecedented number of elite state curators and heritage officials have been felled.

Beyond Xu Huping at the Nanjing Museum, authorities ousted Wang Yamin, the former executive deputy director of the Palace Museum, in 2024. The anti-corruption sweep has also gutted leadership at major provincial tourism and heritage sites: Kang Xiaoming and Du Hua, the former general manager and chairman, respectively, of the Leshan Giant Buddha Tourism Investment Group, were caught in the dragnet in March 2024 and February 2025.

Other high-level museum targets include Jia Jianwei, former director of the Gansu Provincial Museum, and Mou Fengjing, former director of the Chongqing China Three Gorges Museum, both of whom were taken down in May 2025. The purge has continued unabated into the current year, ensnaring former Capital Museum Party Chief Hao Dongchen and Longmen Grottoes Research Academy Director Shi Jiazhen in March 2026.
