China Growth Not Fully Translating Into Market Gains, Former Adviser Says
By Yu HairongPublished: Mar. 19, 2026  12:52 p.m.  GMT+8
00:00/02:02
Listen to this article
Liu Shijin. Photo: IC Photo
Liu Shijin. Photo: IC Photo
China’s economic growth is not fully translating into market-recognized gains, a former senior government adviser said, pointing to persistent deflationary pressures and calling for a shift toward consumption.

Liu Shijin, a former deputy director of the State Council’s Development Research Center, said China should pivot from an investment- and export-driven model toward one led more by consumption and human capital during the 15th Five-Year Plan period starting in 2026.

His remarks, made at a book launch Tuesday, underscore concerns over weak demand in the world’s second-largest economy, where price indicators have remained subdued. 

China’s GDP deflator, a broad measure of overall price levels in the economy, has stayed negative for 11 consecutive quarters through the end of last year, with nominal GDP growth trailing real growth over the same period. 

Liu described the gap as growth not recognized by the market, linking it to excess capacity, rising inventories, increasing payment arrears, higher leverage and declining profitability. 

He said policymakers should consider setting targets to ensure nominal GDP growth does not fall below real growth.

The downturn in property and slowdown in infrastructure investment have exposed a shortfall in consumption, Liu said, noting that consumption lags the international average by about 20 percentage points as a share of GDP.

He warned against launching new large-scale investment projects in sectors already facing overcapacity, saying such moves could temporarily boost output but worsen imbalances over time.

Instead, he called for expanding consumption in areas such as education, health care and elderly care, describing them as both consumption and investment in human capital that could support innovation and long-term growth.

While macroeconomic policy is likely to remain accommodative, Liu said its impact would be limited without deeper structural reforms, which he described as necessary to address underlying economic challenges.

CK Hutchison Unit Demands $2 Billion After Panama Seizes Ports
By Wang XiaoqingPublished: Mar. 9, 2026  4:39 p.m.  GMT+8
00:00/02:05
Listen to this article

CK Hutchison Holdings Ltd. has launched international arbitration to demand at least $2 billion from the Panamanian government after authorities forcibly seized two of its ports along the Panama Canal.

A unit of the Hong Kong conglomerate, Hutchison Ports PPC (PPC), said in a March 6 statement that it will relentlessly pursue full compensation for Panama’s severe breach of contract and anti-investor actions, refusing to settle for merely token remedies.

The dispute threatens to complicate CK Hutchison’s ongoing efforts to sell a massive global port portfolio to a BlackRock Inc.-led consortium, a deal being closely watched amid the potential participation of a Chinese state-owned shipping giant.

The conflict escalated in late February when Panama formally revoked PPC’s operation rights for the Cristobal and Balboa ports on either side of the canal. The move followed a Jan. 29 ruling by the country’s Supreme Court that the company’s concession contract, which was initially signed in 1997 and renewed for 25 years in 2021, was unconstitutional. Authorities assumed administrative and operational control of the terminals on Feb. 23, barring PPC representatives from the sites and terminating its operations.

The arbitration also targets subsequent government actions that PPC characterized as an extreme overreach lacking valid court authorization. On Feb. 26, Panamanian officials forced their way into a private storage facility without notice, seizing legally privileged documents and other proprietary assets unrelated to port operations, the company said.

The two seized terminals were originally part of a package of 43 international ports that CK Hutchison set out to offload in March 2025. Despite the Panamanian government’s takeover, the BlackRock-led consortium is still pushing ahead with negotiations for the remaining 41 ports, with China Cosco Shipping Corp. Ltd. maintaining its involvement in the talks, the Financial Times reported on March 3.

Cover Story: U.S.-Israel War With Iran Drifts Toward Attrition
By Feng Yuding, Hu Xuan, Wang Jing, Wang Kerou, Lu Chen, Luo Zilin and Zeng JiaPublished: Mar. 16, 2026  5:11 p.m.  GMT+8
00:00/06:48
Listen to this article
The U.S.-Israel war with Iran may be drifting toward a war of attrition, as Tehran stabilizes its leadership, and missile strikes continue across the Gulf.
The U.S.-Israel war with Iran may be drifting toward a war of attrition, as Tehran stabilizes its leadership, and missile strikes continue across the Gulf.
As the U.S.-Israeli war with Iran enters its third week, hopes for a quick military victory are fading.

Iran has swiftly installed a new supreme leader, avoided a political breakdown and signaled readiness for a prolonged fight, raising the possibility that a campaign initially expected to end quickly could evolve into a grinding regional standoff.

The war began on Feb. 28 with a U.S.-Israeli strike in Tehran that killed Iran’s longtime supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. The operation — one of the most dramatic decapitation strikes in modern Middle East history — appeared designed to cripple Iran’s leadership and potentially trigger political collapse.

But Tehran has stabilized its leadership, maintained control over its security institutions and made clear it will not accept Washington’s calls for unconditional surrender.

Taken together, those developments suggest the conflict may be entering a new phase — defined less by battlefield breakthroughs than by attrition, regional disruption and mounting risks to the global economy.

Leadership transition

One of the most immediate tests for Iran after the strike was whether the political system could survive the sudden loss of a leader who had dominated the country’s politics for nearly four decades.

That question was answered quickly. On March 8, Iran’s Assembly of Experts elected Mojtaba Khamenei, the late leader’s 56-year-old son, as the Islamic Republic’s third supreme leader.


Mojtaba Khamenei, the second son of the late Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, appears in a video on Iranian state television on March 9. Photo: VCG
While the succession drew criticism from Washington and Israel, the transition appeared to proceed without major institutional disruption. Iran’s powerful Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps swiftly pledged loyalty to the new leader, signaling that the country’s most powerful military force remained aligned with the political leadership.

Analysts said the smooth transition highlights how power in Iran is dispersed across multiple institutions rather than concentrated solely in the supreme leader.

In a March 12 statement — his first since taking office — Mojtaba Khamenei vowed to continue to retaliate and employ strategic leverage to blockade the Strait of Hormuz, while signaling Tehran could increase pressure on its adversaries over time.


People gather to express support for the new Supreme Leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, at Revolution Square in Tehran on March 9. Photo: Abedin Taherkenareh / VCG
War of attrition

Military developments in the first weeks of the conflict suggest the war is shifting toward attrition.

On March 10, U.S. Joint Chiefs of Staff Chairman Dan Caine said American forces had struck more than 5,000 targets inside Iran, including missile launch sites and drone facilities, noting that Iranian ballistic missile launches had fallen about 90% since the start of the war.

But Iranian strikes have not stopped.

Missiles and drones have continued to target Israel as well as U.S. military facilities and civilian infrastructure across the Gulf, including sites in Saudi Arabia, the U.A.E., Qatar, Bahrain, Oman and Kuwait.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said Iran relies on what it calls a “mosaic defense” — a decentralized system designed to allow local military commands to continue operating even if communications with central leadership are disrupted.

Iran has continued to use relatively inexpensive drones alongside missile attacks, allowing it to sustain attacks despite superior U.S. and Israeli airpower.

That asymmetry poses a challenge for Western defenses. An Iranian Shahed drone may cost tens of thousands of dollars, while interceptor missiles used to shoot them down can cost millions.

Such dynamics could stretch the conflict into a costly stalemate by forcing Iran’s adversaries to spend far more on defense than it does on offense.

Tensions in Washington

The prospect of a longer war has exposed tensions within the U.S.

On March 9, U.S. President Donald Trump said the war would end “very soon.” But a day earlier, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said the campaign had only just begun and did not rule out deploying ground troops.


U.S. President Trump stated that he would end the war soon in Miami, Florida on March 9. Photo: Saul Loeb / VCG
The conflicting statements contrast with Trump’s earlier insistence that no deal with Iran would be acceptable short of “unconditional surrender.” By March 10, however, he suggested negotiations with Iran might still be possible under certain conditions.

The mixed signals reflect the political pressures facing Washington as the conflict unfolds.

Rising energy prices have added to the pressure on the Trump administration at home, while the risk of regional escalation could draw additional countries into the conflict. Oil prices surged toward $120 a barrel in early March after shipping through the Strait of Hormuz was severely disrupted, before falling back after Trump said the war could end soon.

At the same time, the U.S. has shown concern about attacks on Iranian oil infrastructure that could further disrupt global energy markets. Media reports said Washington has urged Israel to halt further strikes on Iranian energy facilities, amid fears that a wider energy conflict could aggravate inflation and increase political pressure ahead of U.S. midterm elections.

Widening regional conflict

The war has spread beyond Iran and Israel.

Within hours of the initial strikes, Iran launched retaliatory attacks against Israel and against locations across the Gulf hosting U.S. forces.

Missiles and drones have struck or threatened targets from Tel Aviv to Bahrain, forcing governments across the region to activate air defenses and suspend some civilian air traffic.

Despite the attacks, Gulf states have largely limited their responses to air defenses and diplomatic condemnations rather than direct military retaliation against Iran.

Analysts said those governments face a delicate balancing act. While they oppose Iranian attacks on their territory, they are wary of becoming more deeply entangled in a conflict that threatens regional stability and economic growth.

Global stakes

As the military confrontation unfolds, its broader effects are rippling through the global economy.

Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz — one of the world’s most critical energy chokepoints — remains severely disrupted as insurers and shipping companies reassess the risks of operating in the region. Large oil tankers and container ships have largely avoided the route, although some lower-value vessels continue to use it.


A Thai cargo ship burns after being attacked in the Strait of Hormuz on March 11. Photo: IC photo
Oil prices have surged and financial markets have swung sharply, underscoring how quickly geopolitical tensions in the Persian Gulf can reverberate through global supply chains.

Energy analysts warned that prolonged disruption could tighten global supplies and complicate central bank efforts to contain inflation.

For now, the trajectory of the conflict remains uncertain.

Iran’s rapid leadership transition, its refusal to negotiate with the U.S. and its decentralized military strategy suggest Tehran may be preparing for a confrontation that lasts months not days.

China Fiscal Spending Pace Hits Five-Year High
By Cheng SiweiPublished: Mar. 23, 2026  3:06 p.m.  GMT+8
00:00/01:21
Listen to this article

China front-loaded fiscal spending at the fastest pace for the start of a year in five years, underscoring efforts to support growth even as revenue remained subdued.

Fiscal outlays under the general public budget (GPB) — the largest of the four budgets in China’s fiscal system — rose 3.6% year-on-year to 4.67 trillion yuan ($678 billion) in January-February, according to finance ministry data.

That accounted for 15.6% of the annual GPB, marking the fastest pace of spending for the period in half a decade, according to Caixin calculations.

Revenue under the GPB rose a modest 0.7%, reversing last year’s decline. Tax revenue showed mixed signals: value-added tax rose 4.7%, while other major taxes fell, partly due to base effects and the later timing of the Lunar New Year. Strong growth in trade-related taxes pointed to solid foreign trade performance.


Separately, the real estate downturn continued to weigh on government-managed fund revenue, with land sales revenue — a key funding source for local authorities — falling 25.2%.

Expenditures from government-managed funds rose 16%, likely backed by accelerated issuance of local government special-purpose bonds.

Analysis: Did Quant Funds Amplify China’s Market Rout?
By Yue YueMar 23, 2026 11:48 PM
00:00/02:02
Listen to this article
China’s sharpest stock market drop of 2026 has revived a key question for investors: did the country’s fast-growing quant funds amplify the sell-off, or merely follow it?

Chinese mainland equities tumbled Monday as a fourth week of escalating Middle East tensions and a broader global market slide weighed on sentiment. The benchmark Shanghai Composite Index fell 3.6%. About 4.3 trillion yuan ($625 billion) was wiped from the mainland market.

Losses were widespread, with more than 5,100 stocks declining and over 100 hitting their daily limit down, highlighting the breadth and speed of the downturn.


Analysts cited external shocks as the main triggers, including rising geopolitical risks and a more hawkish U.S. Federal Reserve that has dampened global liquidity expectations and pressured risk assets. Other Asian markets also fell sharply, reinforcing a risk-off tone.

Yet the sharp underperformance of smaller stocks has drawn attention to domestic market structure. The Wind Micro Market Cap Index, which tracks 400 of the smallest-cap mainland-listed companies, dropped 6.4%, lagging large-cap benchmarks, in moves that aligned with strategies favored by quant funds.

This has fueled debate over whether quant strategies — often built on similar signals and risk controls — may have amplified the decline. Critics say synchronized stop-loss selling can accelerate losses during periods of stress.

Industry participants, however, say such strategies are largely reactive, pointing to broader pressures including fund redemptions, margin selling, and strains in hybrid “fixed-income plus” funds — which combine bonds with equity exposure — that can trigger forced deleveraging. 

With quant trading accounting for a sizable share of daily turnover in the mainland market despite relatively small holdings, the latest rout is likely to intensify scrutiny of how such strategies behave in volatile markets.

Analysis: Chinese Milk Tea Makers Push Farther Afield as Southeast Asia Gets Crowded
By Yang JingMar 23, 2026 06:08 AM
00:00/05:22
Listen to this article
Chinese tea chain Chagee Holdings is preparing to open its first store in South Korea, becoming the latest mainland beverage brand to enter a market that is drawing growing interest as expansion in Southeast Asia becomes more competitive.

The move would make Chagee the newest entrant in South Korea among Chinese tea brands including Heytea, Mixue, ChaPanda and Auntea Jenny. For an industry that once saw Southeast Asia as the obvious first stop overseas, Japan, South Korea, the U.S., Europe and Central Asia are increasingly emerging as the next targets.

The change in direction comes as Chinese tea chains look for fresh growth beyond a region that has already absorbed a wave of aggressive store openings. The global market for new-style tea drinks is expected to grow at a compound annual rate of 7.2% from 2023 to 2028, according to a white paper released by the China New Tea Beverage Industry Alliance and China Insights Consultancy. Southeast Asia remains the fastest-growing major market, with projected annual growth of 19.8%, followed by China at 17.6%.


Southeast Asia has long been the preferred beachhead for Chinese food and beverage brands going abroad. As of Dec. 31, 2024, more than 60 Chinese food and beverage brands had opened over 6,100 outlets across Southeast Asia, according to a report by Singapore-based consultancy Momentum Works. Much of that store count was driven by chains such as Mixue, Chagee and Luckin Coffee.

But the region is getting more crowded, especially in tea drinks, where competition is shifting from rapid expansion to store optimization and operational discipline. In its 2025 interim report, Mixue Group said it was adjusting and optimizing some outlets in Indonesia and Vietnam, and that store numbers in both markets fell during the six months ended June 30, 2025.

Even so, the company said it remained focused on Southeast Asia while also expanding into Central Asia, where it opened its first store in Kazakhstan. Mixue operated about 4,700 stores outside mainland China as of June 30, 2025.

Challenges in Japan, South Korea

Japan and South Korea present a different kind of opportunity. Consumers in both countries already have established tea-drinking habits, and younger customers tend to be open to new products and brands. Sichuan Baicha Baidao Industrial Co. Ltd., operator of ChaPanda, said in its interim report that it had opened 21 overseas stores as of June 30, 2025, including 11 in South Korea.

Still, those markets come with their own challenges. Taiwanese bubble-tea brands established themselves in Japan and South Korea earlier, meaning that mainland chains would have to compete in markets where consumer habits are already shaped and brand education remains necessary, according to a report by marketing agency Meetsocial.

In both Japan and South Korea, the market is still dominated by low- and midpriced brands, with stores concentrated in busy shopping districts and student-heavy neighborhoods, it said.

That could make novelty harder to sustain. The Korea Herald reported in September that Taiwanese brands Gong Cha and Tiger Sugar entered South Korea well before the latest wave of Chinese competitors, but both have recently struggled.

Gong Cha’s South Korean store count declined in 2024 for the first time, though it still had 898 outlets. Tiger Sugar’s footprint shrank from 52 stores in 2020 to 13 by September 2025. The report said newer entrants such as Chagee and Heytea still need to prove they can hold consumer interest in a country where coffee remains the dominant drink.

Expanding West

Chinese tea brands are also pushing farther into Western markets. In 2025, Nayuki, Mixue and Auntea Jenny each opened their first stores in the U.S. Molly Tea opened its first store in the U.K., while ChaPanda launched in Spain and France.


The U.S. has become one of the industry’s most closely watched markets. The U.S. market for freshly made tea drinks is projected to reach $2.9 billion by 2029, according to industry tracker Hongcan.

The number of freshly made tea merchants in the country rose 18.2% in 2025 from a year earlier to 7,845. In New York’s Flushing neighborhood, where a large Chinese community has helped support demand, a cluster of chains including ChaPanda, Molly Tea, Auntea Jenny, Nayuki and Heytea has turned one commercial strip into what Chinese media describe as a “milk tea street.”

Among the newer brands drawing attention is Molly Tea. The chain added more than 1,100 stores in 2025, lifting its store count by 101.3%, according to a report released jointly by the company and Hongcan. The company chose Los Angeles for its first overseas market and more than a year after opening, the store was generating stable monthly gross merchandise volume of about $500,000, Southern Metropolis Daily reported in November. It has since expanded to New York, San Francisco, Seattle and Philadelphia.
