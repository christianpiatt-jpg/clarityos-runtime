Intel is joining Terafab, the ambitious chipmaking project announced last month by Elon Musk. Shares in the California-based chip giant rose early Tuesday on the news. Terafab is a joint venture between Musk's companies SpaceX, xAI and Tesla that's meant to unite all the stages of the chipmaking process "under one roof," and churn out chips for Optimus robots and SpaceX satellites faster than other companies have been able to so far. Musk has touted the $20 billion-plus project as "the most epic chip-building exercise in history by far."

I called this one. Two weeks ago, I published my analysis of Elon Musk’s Terafab and wrote: “My best guess: Terafab becomes an advanced technology development center that partners with Samsung Semiconductor, Intel, or both for the actual high-volume manufacturing on leading-edge nodes.”

This morning, Intel announced it is joining Terafab as a strategic partner alongside Tesla, SpaceX, and xAI. Intel is contributing what it calls “industry-leading semiconductor process technology and advanced packaging expertise.” That directly addresses what I identified as the single biggest open question from the March 21 announcement: whose process technology?

A few things worth noting.

First, this validates the thesis that chip design and chip manufacturing are different businesses. Musk proved he can design competitive silicon. Now he’s done the smart thing and partnered with a company that has decades of manufacturing DNA rather than trying to build that capability from zero.

Second, Intel Foundry has been aggressively pursuing external customers since Lip-Bu Tan took over as CEO. The NVIDIA custom chip partnership in September 2025 was an early signal. Terafab is another. 

Both sides get what they need: Musk gets a process technology partner, Intel gets volume commitments that justify continued investment in advanced nodes.

Third, back in August 2025, I noted that Tesla’s chip strategy appeared to be a Tesla-Intel-Samsung arrangement. Eight months later, that arrangement has formalized into something much bigger than Dojo3 chips.

What I’m still watching for: specifics on which Intel process node Terafab will use, the order timelines for ASML, KLA, and Applied Materials equipment, and how this partnership interacts with Tesla’s existing $16.5 billion Samsung deal for AI6 chips through 2033. The partnership is real. 

The execution timeline is still an open question.

I’m not convinced Tesla’s Terafab vision will truly succeed — even 10 years from now.

That’s not because I underestimate Tesla.
It’s because I think too many people underestimate how brutally difficult the semiconductor industry really is.

If Elon Musk had fully seen the AI wave coming from the beginning, Tesla probably would have moved into chip manufacturing much earlier.
Instead, Tesla spent years designing chips while relying on outside foundries for production.

To me, that says something important.

This does not look like the execution of a long-mastered strategy.
It looks more like a late realization:

AI is scaling faster than expected, chips are becoming too strategic to leave in other people’s hands, and now Tesla wants deeper control.

That logic is understandable.
Any company betting its future on AI would want tighter control over compute, supply chain, and production capacity.

But semiconductors are not EVs.

EVs gave room for speed, disruption, capital, and bold execution.
Semiconductors — especially advanced-node manufacturing — do not work that way.

You do not build real semiconductor capability just because you have money.
You do not create a world-class fab just because you hire smart engineers.
And you definitely do not compress decades of manufacturing learning into a few years just because a founder is ambitious.

This industry runs on accumulated process knowledge, painful yield learning, manufacturing discipline, supplier integration, failure analysis, packaging know-how, and years of repetition.

That is why I remain skeptical.

Many semiconductor engineers are not driven by the same “let’s build the future” startup energy that helped reshape EVs.
They tend to ask much harder, more practical questions:
 • Can this actually reach stable mass production?
 • Can yield be improved and sustained?
 • Will leadership stay committed when the learning curve gets ugly?
 • Can the organization handle years of slow, expensive, unglamorous process improvement?

That is the real test.

Founder-driven intensity can absolutely move industries.
But semiconductor manufacturing is one of the few fields where vision alone is nowhere near enough.

Wanting to build chips is not the same as being able to build chips.

Tesla may absolutely build something meaningful.
It may create stronger in-house chip capability.
It may secure parts of the stack that matter strategically.

But becoming a true manufacturing force at the highest level?
That is a very different challenge.

And that is why, even 10 years from now, I am not yet convinced Terafab becomes the success story many people expect.

Semiconductors are not built by ambition alone.
They are built by patience, repetition, scars, and manufacturing reality.

That is what makes this so interesting to watch.
