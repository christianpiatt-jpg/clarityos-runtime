China Youth Jobless Rate Falls to 16.1%, Extending Decline
By Xia YiningPublished: Mar. 19, 2026  10:47 p.m.  GMT+8
00:00/02:43
Listen to this article
A spring job fair in Guangzhou in 2026. Photo: VCG
A spring job fair in Guangzhou in 2026. Photo: VCG
China’s unemployment rate for youth ages 16 to 24 fell to 16.1% in February, marking an eighth straight month of decline.

The sustained drop in youth joblessness — a measure that excludes enrolled students — diverges from typical seasonal patterns and offers a bright spot in a labor market where unemployment among older groups ticked up slightly due to holiday effects.

The National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) said Thursday that the jobless rate for those ages 16 to 24 fell 0.2 percentage points from January, extending a downward trend that began in August 2025. The February reading was 0.8 percentage points lower than a year earlier. 

By contrast, the unemployment rate for those ages 25 to 29 rose 0.4 percentage points to 7.2%, while the rate for those ages 30 to 59 edged up 0.2 percentage points to 4.2%. These increases may have been influenced by the Spring Festival holiday. Both figures remained slightly below their levels in February 2025.

The nationwide urban surveyed unemployment rate stood at 5.3% in February, up 0.1 percentage point from a month earlier. Average weekly working hours for enterprise employees fell 0.2 hours to 48.1 hours, the lowest level since March 2025, a shift also likely driven primarily by the holiday period. Fu Linghui, a spokesperson for the NBS, said March 16 that the average urban unemployment rate for the first two months of the year was 5.3%, unchanged from a year earlier, suggesting overall employment conditions remained stable.

China revised its employment data methodology in December 2023, removing enrolled students from the youth unemployment calculation and splitting the 25-to-59 age group into two subgroups. Based on trends observed in 2024 and 2025, youth unemployment typically shows strong seasonality, rising briefly during the Spring Festival period, declining through the spring, and peaking sharply in July and August as new graduates enter the labor force. The continued decline in January and February 2026 breaks from the usual holiday-related uptick.

Youth joblessness remains elevated relative to overall workforce levels globally. In the U.S., the seasonally adjusted unemployment rate for those ages 16 to 24 was 9.5% in February 2026, above the overall rate of 4.4%. In the European Union, January 2026 data showed an under-25 unemployment rate of 15.1%, compared with a general unemployment rate of 5.8%.

In Depth: China Tackles Risks in Legacy PPP Projects
By Cheng Siwei and Yu HairongMar 19, 2026 03:38 AM
00:00/08:01
Listen to this article
China’s public-private partnership model, which expanded rapidly from 2014 to drive infrastructure growth, has come under strain, with projects facing delayed government payments and growing concerns among lenders.
China’s public-private partnership model, which expanded rapidly from 2014 to drive infrastructure growth, has come under strain, with projects facing delayed government payments and growing concerns among lenders.
In the final days of December, a series of exchange filings from M-Grass Ecology And Environment (Group) Co. Ltd. offered a glimpse into China’s broader effort to address risks in legacy public-private partnership (PPP) projects.

The Shenzhen-listed company disclosed it was terminating four PPP projects ahead of schedule and restructuring their debt.

These moves are among the results of a sweeping new directive from Beijing aimed at confronting the more than 10 trillion yuan ($1.4 trillion) overhang of legacy PPP projects. The PPP model, which expanded rapidly from 2014 to drive infrastructure growth, has come under strain, with projects facing delayed government payments and growing concerns among lenders. By late 2024, banks had grown increasingly wary of their exposure, with some projects facing the risk of funding cuts, Caixin reported previously.

In August, Beijing rolled out a framework, Guideline 84, designed to prevent the country’s portfolio of over 10,000 legacy projects from posing broader financial risks.

Beijing’s blueprint

PPPs were intended to ease local fiscal burdens by attracting private capital for public works. In practice, however, many were used as de facto financing channels, with governments bearing significant payment obligations. In recent years, some projects have faced delays, payment arrears and rising disputes.

Critically, Guideline 84 explicitly allows local governments to use funds from both general and special-purpose bonds (SPBs) to cover their share of construction costs.

It also sends a clear message to banks. The guideline instructs financial institutions to “actively support financing for projects under construction in accordance with market-oriented and law-based principles.” It urges them to streamline credit approval and warns them not to terminate financing without reason or demand commitment letters from local governments.

One of the guideline’s most crucial elements is a call for a broad negotiation between government authorities, private-sector partners and lenders — to adjust project scope, contract duration, interest rates and profit metrics.

Wang Shouqing, chief expert at Tsinghua University’s Research Center for Investment and Financing Policy, said the new policy provides a lifeline for the faltering sector. Speaking at a forum in November, he noted that Guideline 84 encourages all parties to take a step back, which helps alleviate fears among officials of being held responsible for the loss of state assets, and provides a compliant path to introduce or raise user fees.

Since the directive’s release, multiple industry insiders told Caixin that market activity has picked up, with local governments and private companies initiating discussions. The investment viability of these assets is changing, and some institutions are now willing to discuss taking them over.

Provincial plans

While Guideline 84 provides the national strategy, the work of implementation is falling to the provinces, which have begun to roll out their own tailored plans.

Yunnan and Gansu have emerged as early movers. Yunnan holds the largest PPP investment value in the country and was hit hard during a nationwide cleanup of the PPP project database that began in 2017. According to its finance department, from 2016 to 2020, Yunnan had 513 projects worth 1.34 trillion yuan, with highway infrastructure accounting for nearly 74% of the investment value.

In a move that caught the market’s attention, Yunnan’s plan introduced a novel exit route: reclassifying certain projects as direct government investments. This applies to projects that lack “core PPP elements,” such as those that lack substantive operations, generate no revenue or are strongly public-service oriented.

For private companies, it can be an attractive off-ramp. The biggest problem for some companies right now is cash flow, said Qu Jiangfeng, board secretary of Xingtai Road and Bridge Construction Group Co. Ltd. Many construction companies originally participated in PPPs to drive their core business, not because they wanted to own or operate the project long-term, Qu told Caixin.

The case of M-Grass Ecology underscores this. The early termination of its four projects was explicitly framed as a way to take advantage of debt-resolution policies introduced by central and Inner Mongolian authorities to recover cash as soon as possible. In three of the four debt restructurings, some project-related debt was forgiven.

The SPB solution

Caixin has learned from multiple sources that since Guideline 84 was announced, provinces like Yunnan have begun using SPBs to cover government arrears on PPP projects. Others, like Zhejiang, have gone further, using SPBs to completely replace the financing for some legacy PPPs, effectively converting them into SPB projects.

However, the path lacks a unified set of guidelines. Many local governments want to use SPBs to solve their legacy PPP problems, but they urgently need to know how to apply, said a government investment researcher. According to a team at Jingtian & Gongcheng law firm, legal ambiguities remain, such as whether SPB funds can cover only construction costs or also early termination compensation fees.

Another constraint is the scale of the problem — the more than 10 trillion yuan in projects dwarfs the available funds. The 2025 new SPB quota was 4.4 trillion yuan, a level maintained this year. But SPBs serve multiple priorities, including shoring up government finances, resolving hidden debt and paying arrears to businesses.

Renegotiating contracts

There is a broad consensus among industry players that the high returns and interest rates agreed upon during the PPP boom of 2015-2018 are no longer tenable in the current low-rate environment. Guideline 84 has provided policy support for this process, but it remains a delicate negotiation.

The policy direction is to encourage government authorities, private-sector partners and lenders to negotiate on cost reduction and efficiency improvement, but the original PPP contracts are legally binding, said Tang Feng, a deputy director of the Nanchang innovation base of the Chinese Academy of Fiscal Sciences.

According to a person at a company in a northern city, some of these talks have been underway for years. “During the PPP boom years of 2015 to 2017, project financing was generally priced at or above the benchmark rate of 4.9%,” he said. “Now, most projects can be negotiated to below 3%.”

While many commercial banks have been willing to discuss extensions and rate cuts, some policy banks have been more cautious, according to market sources. Some new PPP projects are securing loans at 50 basis points below the loan prime rate (LPR), while even better-quality legacy projects are still paying the LPR or higher after a round of cuts, said Cai Fangfang, a manager at Shanghai Jumbo Consulting Co. Ltd. This, she argued, shows there is still room for legacy financing costs to fall.

Beyond financial cuts, private partners are also being urged to innovate. The guideline calls on them to introduce advanced technology, improve management, and find new revenue streams to boost project profitability. But this also faces hurdles. As one PPP consultant told Caixin, some projects have underperformed on revenue because initial projections were inflated to meet policy requirements at the time. In other cases, revenue sources that exist on paper have been impossible to collect due to a lack of coordination between different government departments.

Despite the flurry of new policies, many provinces are still in the preliminary stage of conducting comprehensive reviews of their legacy projects. Only after this stock-taking is complete will the next phase of renegotiation and cost reduction begin, sources with knowledge of the matter told Caixin.

China Growth Not Fully Translating Into Market Gains, Former Adviser Says
By Yu HairongMar 19, 2026 00:52 AM
00:00/02:02
Listen to this article
Liu Shijin. Photo: IC Photo
Liu Shijin. Photo: IC Photo
China’s economic growth is not fully translating into market-recognized gains, a former senior government adviser said, pointing to persistent deflationary pressures and calling for a shift toward consumption.

Liu Shijin, a former deputy director of the State Council’s Development Research Center, said China should pivot from an investment- and export-driven model toward one led more by consumption and human capital during the 15th Five-Year Plan period starting in 2026.

His remarks, made at a book launch Tuesday, underscore concerns over weak demand in the world’s second-largest economy, where price indicators have remained subdued. 

China’s GDP deflator, a broad measure of overall price levels in the economy, has stayed negative for 11 consecutive quarters through the end of last year, with nominal GDP growth trailing real growth over the same period. 

Liu described the gap as growth not recognized by the market, linking it to excess capacity, rising inventories, increasing payment arrears, higher leverage and declining profitability. 

He said policymakers should consider setting targets to ensure nominal GDP growth does not fall below real growth.

The downturn in property and slowdown in infrastructure investment have exposed a shortfall in consumption, Liu said, noting that consumption lags the international average by about 20 percentage points as a share of GDP.

He warned against launching new large-scale investment projects in sectors already facing overcapacity, saying such moves could temporarily boost output but worsen imbalances over time.

Instead, he called for expanding consumption in areas such as education, health care and elderly care, describing them as both consumption and investment in human capital that could support innovation and long-term growth.

While macroeconomic policy is likely to remain accommodative, Liu said its impact would be limited without deeper structural reforms, which he described as necessary to address underlying economic challenges.

Analysis: China’s Plan to Tackle Weak Demand, Low Inflation
By Fan Qianchan and Cheng SiweiMar 18, 2026 06:42 AM
00:00/06:34
Listen to this article
China has set its 2026 GDP growth target as a range for only the third time, in a deliberate strategy to build flexibility into its economic management.
China has set its 2026 GDP growth target as a range for only the third time, in a deliberate strategy to build flexibility into its economic management.
As China embarks on its 15th Five-Year Plan in 2026, its leadership is grappling with a complex economic landscape marked by growing uncertainty abroad and domestic demand that has not kept up with supply.

How to navigate these challenges with proactive macroeconomic policies became the central focus of this year’s top annual political meetings known as the “Two Sessions,” where Premier Li Qiang announced on March 5 that the country’s GDP growth target for 2026 had been set at a range of 4.5% to 5%.

The move, which marks only the third time China has set its annual growth target as a range, is a deliberate strategy to build flexibility into its economic management. Li said the target primarily aims to leave room for structural adjustments, risk prevention and reform promotion in the opening year of the new five-year plan, in order to lay a solid foundation for growth later on.

The range does more than create breathing room to deal with the variety of uncertainties, said Shen Danyang, director of the State Council’s Research Office. It also allows local governments to set their own growth targets based on local conditions. The hope is that it will encourage all levels of government to focus on high-quality economic development.

This flexible target is the centerpiece of a broader economic blueprint for the year, one that relies on a combination of proactive fiscal spending and coordinated structural reforms to boost domestic demand, improve public welfare, and steer the economy toward a more sustainable growth model.


Unpacking the plan

China’s more flexible GDP target for this year is a sign that it aims to tackle underlying economic pressures like low prices and weak domestic demand.

• The target takes into account the need to leave room for adjusting the structure of the economy this year, while remaining in line with the economic goals that policymakers aim to reach by 2035, said Shen.

• It also offers greater flexibility by taking some of the pressure off local governments to single-mindedly pursue high GDP growth, said Dong Yu, executive deputy dean of Tsinghua University’s China Institute for Development Planning.

A central part of the plan is tackling low inflation by setting a consumer price increase target of “around 2%,” a move intended to stimulate a healthier economic cycle.

• The strategy involves using macroeconomic policies to expand domestic demand, boost consumption and expand effective investment, while also deepening supply-side reforms and managing price competition, government researcher Chen Changsheng said.

• The government’s budget implies nominal GDP growth of 5% — slightly higher than the real GDP target — reflecting the official intent to lift prices, according to financial institutions like Guotai Haitong Securities Co. Ltd. and Industrial Securities Co. Ltd.

Expanding domestic demand remains a top priority, with a clear focus on boosting personal income and encouraging consumer spending.

• The policy focus for 2026 has shifted toward increasing income and providing financial support, including a 100 billion yuan ($1.4 billion) fiscal-financial package to promote domestic demand, said Xiong Yuan, Guosheng Securities Co. Ltd.’s chief economist.

• Support measures include raising the minimum basic pension, providing nearly 100 billion yuan in childcare subsidies and introducing spring and autumn school breaks to give people more opportunities to spend.

• The central government will arrange 250 billion yuan in ultra-long-term special treasury bonds to support the trade-in of consumer goods, a decrease from last year that economist Luo Zhiheng believes reflects narrowing demand for durable goods after two years of similar programs.

Spending breakdown

For 2026, the plan is to maintain a proactive fiscal stance that keeps deficits at historically high levels.

• The official deficit-to-GDP ratio has been set around 4% for the second straight year, amounting to a nearly 5.9 trillion yuan deficit, with the entire increase coming from the central government.

• The broad deficit, which includes other government borrowing, will reach almost 13.9 trillion yuan, a relatively restrained increase from last year, according to Yuan Haixia, head of the research institute at China Chengxin International Credit Rating Co. Ltd.

• Standard Chartered Bank economist Ding Shuang estimated the broad deficit will be around 8% to 9% of GDP, compared with 9% in 2025.

Beyond the headline deficit, fiscal support this year is also coming from “quasi-fiscal” tools.

• The government will arrange 800 billion yuan in a policy-backed financing instrument, an increase of 300 billion yuan from last year and a move that GF Securities Co. Ltd. Chief Economist Guo Lei sees as a clear sign of the government’s pro-investment stance.

• This tool will be a main source of new funds for investment in 2026, said Lu Ting, chief China economist at Nomura Holdings Inc.

To ensure investment funds are not diverted, China will separately list and increase the quota for local government special-purpose bonds that are used for project construction, a move Guo said will stop debt repayments from crowding out investment.

At the same time, the government is continuing a shift in its spending structure toward “investing in people” and improving livelihoods, as opposed to a singular focus on investment.

• Spending on health care, education, senior care and housing support is expected to account for nearly 40% of the 2026 general public budget, according to economist Luo.

Coordinating policy

China is strengthening the link between its reform agenda and macroeconomic policies to unclog economic bottlenecks and create growth that can sustain itself.

• A key initiative is the 100 billion yuan fiscal-financial package that uses tools like loan interest subsidies and financing guarantees to support private investment and consumer spending, said Finance Minister Lan Fo’an.

• Preliminary calculations suggest that the 100 billion yuan in fiscal funds can support trillions of yuan in credit.

The reform agenda also includes drafting new regulations to build a unified national market, a move aimed at regulating local governments and breaking down local protectionism, said Yuan of China Chengxin.

• To boost business confidence, the government plans to improve supporting regulations for its private economy law and put more effort into resolving the issue of arrears owed to companies by local governments.

• Other reforms include improving the local tax system, overhauling consumption taxes, and increasing the share of profits collected from state-owned enterprises to bolster government funds.
