Below is a clear conceptual "trust–coherence curve" you can use to design and evaluate hydronic tension. I describe the axes, key regions, decision thresholds, what to measure at each point, and practical design/response actions you can take to keep an audience inside the productive tension zone.

1) Axes and basic shape
- X axis: Coherence (match between expectation and experienced reality). Ranges from 0 (complete incoherence) to 1 (perfect coherence).  
- Y axis: Sustained tension (hydronic tension / sustained arousal). Rises from low at extremes to a peak in the mid‑coherence range; shape is an inverted-U (low at very low and very high coherence, maximal at intermediate coherence).

2) Key regions along the curve
- High coherence (0.75–1.0): Predictable, trusted context. Tension low because expectations are met reliably; arousal minimal. Good for anchor/deposit phases.  
- Productive tension band (approx. 0.4–0.75): Partial, recoverable violations of expectation. Reappraisal required but trust remains reparable; this is the optimal zone for sustained hydronic tension.  
- Collapse region (0–0.4): Coherence so low that epistemic trust breaks; viewers disengage or interpret violations as errors/deception, causing tension and engagement to drop.

3) Critical thresholds and operational rules
- Anchor threshold (≥0.75): Use early to establish genre/trust. Provide sufficient cues so the audience accepts the frame.  
- Perturbation entry (~0.75 → 0.6): First planned violations should lower coherence into the productive band without dropping below ~0.4.  
- Repair threshold (~0.4): If coherence dips below this, initiate rapid repair signals; otherwise expect disengagement.  
- Meta‑coherence rule: Over sessions, keep the average coherence above the collapse threshold while allowing within‑session fluctuations that traverse the productive band.

4) Measurement proxies mapped to regions
- Coherence estimate: compute label-match rate (expected label vs. viewer label) and narrative plausibility score from participant ratings. Normalize to 0–1.  
- Tension/engagement: continuous physiological (skin conductance, HRV), continuous subjective arousal ratings, and behavioral engagement (view time, micro‑pauses).  
- Collapse indicators: sustained drop in view time, lack of recovery in physiology after perturbation, qualitative reports of “confusion” or “feeling misled.”

5) Design actions tied to curve position
- If coherence >0.75 (low tension): introduce controlled ambiguity (small perturbation), incrementally reduce coherence into 0.4–0.75.  
- If coherence in 0.4–0.75 (productive): vary the size and timing of violations; use linguistic labels and repair cues to prolong reappraisal cycles without breaching the repair threshold.  
- If coherence <0.4 (danger): deploy rapid repair: clarifying language, narrative justification, restore familiar motifs, explicit framing cues. If repair fails, consider a temporary pause or meta‑commentary to re‑establish trust.

6) Temporal dynamics and budgeting
- Trust budget: treat coherence reserves like currency. Early anchors deposit trust; each violation spends it. Schedule larger violations only after fresh deposits or after successful small repairs.  
- Cycle timing: short perturbation–repair cycles (seconds to minutes) sustain tension; long unresolved incoherence (minutes+) risks collapse.

7) Example application (micro‑sequence)
- Deposit phase: establish anchor with clear genre cue and repeated ritual phrase (coherence ~0.85).  
- Perturbation: present ambiguous expression + misleading soundtrack (coherence drops to ~0.6). Viewers’ arousal rises.  
- Repair: immediately follow with a reframing line or shot that plausibly explains the ambiguity (coherence returns to ~0.7). Tension remains elevated but sustainable.

8) Using the curve in evaluation
- Predefine acceptable coherence band (e.g., 0.45–0.75) for your project. Collect coherence proxies in pilot runs and compute time‑in‑band. Aim for high time‑in‑band with frequent safe perturbation–repair cycles.  
- Track moments when coherence dips below threshold and annotate what cue produced the dip; use that to adjust cue magnitude, labeling, or pacing.

9) Ethical and interpretive notes
- The curve assumes audience consent to playful or crafted mismatch. For research or public media, ensure audience understands the mediated nature when necessary to prevent harmful real‑world belief changes.  
- The target band may shift by population; calibrate with your intended audience.
